What's Happening?

The Village of Lemont Planning & Zoning Commission reviews proposed changes to how land can be used in our community. A rezoning proposal has been submitted that affects properties near residential neighborhoods in our area, including the Estates of Montefiori and surrounding single-family home communities.

Rezoning changes what types of buildings and activities are allowed on specific parcels of land. These decisions can have lasting impacts on neighboring areas, affecting traffic patterns, property values, and the character of our established residential communities.

What is Rezoning?

Rezoning is the process of changing the designated use of a property from one zoning classification to another. For example, land currently zoned for single-family homes might be rezoned for multi-family housing, commercial use, or other purposes. This change affects what can be built, how tall buildings can be, parking requirements, and more.

Critical Concerns About This Development

As residents review this proposal, several major issues have emerged that raise serious questions about the developer's plans and projections.

The Numbers Don't Add Up

The developer's claims about families, school enrollment, and population density contradict the actual apartment sizes and development scale. These inconsistencies deserve careful scrutiny.

🏢

Extreme Density on Limited Land

13 apartment buildings (60 units) on just 5.3 acres = 9.45 units per acre. This represents an unprecedented level of density for our area. For comparison:

  • Nearby Beechen & Dill development (127th St): 82 units on 29 acres = only 2.82 units/acre
  • The undersized lot requirement (4,000 vs 10,000 sq ft) is the primary driver of this excessive density
  • This level of crowding will strain local infrastructure and dramatically change neighborhood character
❓

Rental Development, Not Owner-Occupied

This will be a rental property development. While the developer plans to keep 51% and potentially sell 49%, the residents living here will be renters, not owners. This means:

  • No pride of ownership from actual residents
  • Higher turnover rates typical of rental properties
  • Less investment in maintaining property appearance and community standards
  • Potential for absentee landlord issues if buildings are sold to investors
👨‍👩‍👧

"Built for Families" Doesn't Match Reality

The units are 1-bedroom apartments with 1 den, renting for $1,500-$2,000/month. The developer claims these are designed for families, but the small unit size contradicts this. These are clearly designed as rental units, not family homes.

🎒

Unrealistic School Impact Estimate

The developer estimates only 3.5 children total for the entire development. With an estimated 100 new residents moving in, claiming less than 4 school-age children is highly unrealistic. This appears to be an attempt to minimize concerns about overcrowding local schools.

🚗

Insufficient Parking

Only 1.5 parking spots per unit (meets code minimum but provides ZERO guest parking). For rental apartments housing potentially 100+ residents, this creates serious problems:

  • No designated guest parking for visitors, deliveries, or service vehicles
  • Overflow parking will spill into surrounding residential streets
  • Creates congestion and safety issues for existing homeowners
  • Meeting minimum code requirements doesn't mean adequate parking for real-world use
🚫

Developer Not Listening to Community

Despite resident concerns, the developer continues to push forward with minimal changes. The community has voiced legitimate objections about density, traffic, and neighborhood impact, but these concerns are being dismissed rather than addressed.

Key Questions to Ask at the Meeting

  • How can you claim this is designed for families when the units are only 1-bedroom plus a den?
  • What is the actual ownership structure? Who will own the 49% being sold off?
  • How did you calculate only 3.5 school-age children for 100 new residents?
  • Why is 1.5 parking spots per unit sufficient when similar developments show much higher vehicle counts?
  • How will 13 buildings on 5.3 acres not create severe overcrowding?
  • What traffic studies have been conducted for the intersection of Archer and Bell?
  • How will this development impact property values of surrounding single-family homes?

Official Village Reviews Expose Major Problems

The Village of Lemont's own professional staff have identified serious deficiencies in this proposal. These aren't just resident concerns—these are documented findings from official engineering reviews, traffic studies, and planning analyses.

📋 All Facts Sourced From Official Documents

Village of Lemont UDO Planning Review • HR Green Engineering Review • Lemont Fire District Review • Traffic Impact Study Review • Revised Preliminary Site Plan (March 2026)

⚠️ Massive Zoning Violations

The project fails 6 out of 8 R-6 zoning standards. This isn't a minor adjustment—the proposal requires formal exceptions for:

  • MINIMUM LOT SIZE (MOST EGREGIOUS): Only 4,000 sq ft vs 10,000 sq ft required — 60% BELOW minimum! This is the primary driver of the excessive density. For comparison, a nearby development (Beechen & Dill, 127th St) requested a reduction from 10,000 to only 9,600 sq ft — nowhere near this extreme.
  • Lot area per unit: 946 sq ft proposed vs 2,500 sq ft required (only 38% of minimum!)
  • Side yard setback: 12.5 ft vs 15 ft required
  • Rear yard setback: 15-19 ft vs 30 ft required
  • Building spacing: 25 ft vs 30 ft required
  • Minimum lot width: 75.75 ft vs 80 ft required

Village Planning Review (Feb 13, 2026): When a project requires exceptions to nearly every dimensional standard, that classification is wrong for this site.

💧 Stormwater Threatens Neighboring Homes

HR Green Engineering identified unresolved drainage issues:

  • Stormwater outfall drains toward the north—toward the retention pond near where Colline ends
  • Overflow routing has not been adequately addressed
  • Village needs to see where drainage goes before approval
  • Retaining wall planned on eastern property boundary (adjacent to Montefiori) with no height, material, or drainage specifications provided
  • Effect on adjacent property drainage has not been evaluated

HR Green Engineering Review (Feb 25, 2026): Stormwater overflow route is unresolved.

🏠 Buildings Too Close to Existing Homes

Rear yard setback violation for existing building on site:

  • Existing building: Under 19 ft rear setback vs 30 ft required—over 11 ft shortfall
  • This existing structure will remain as part of the development
  • Located close to the rear property line adjacent to existing detached single-family homes
  • No buffer zone between multi-family development and single-family subdivision

Village Planning Review (Feb 13, 2026): Recommended park or open space buffer to separate multi-family from single-family homes—not incorporated.

🚗 Documented Traffic Safety Hazards

Bell Road has a 4%+ grade—officially flagged as a safety concern:

  • Traffic reviewer stated on-street parking on a 4%+ grade is "a challenge, especially in the snow"
  • Developer has removed the parallel parking on Bell Road and incorporated it into the development
  • Existing crash history at Archer/Bell intersection involving rear-end and turning collisions
  • Horizontal curves and sight distance limitations already documented
  • Bell Road's grade and geometry remain safety concerns for increased traffic volume

Traffic Impact Study Review (Jan 9, 2026): Safety concerns documented for this intersection.

🚧 Critical Engineering Deficiencies

HR Green Engineering Review identified unresolved problems:

  • Road width: Bell Road is 23-24 ft wide; requires 27-33 ft minimum
  • Parking stalls: 18 ft by scale; requires 22-23 ft
  • Sidewalk: Only one side of Bell Road; requires both sides
  • Street lighting: None shown anywhere in development—required
  • Road ownership: Maintenance responsibilities entirely unresolved
  • Multiple permits outstanding: MWRD, IEPA, IDOT, Cook County

HR Green Engineering (Feb 25, 2026): Future platting discussions cannot begin until road ownership is resolved.

🚫 Federal Requirements Violated

ADA Compliance—Federally Required, Currently Absent:

  • No ADA-accessible parking stalls shown in either on-street or internal parking areas
  • On-street Bell Road parking subject to PROWAG requirements—none currently shown
  • This is a federal requirement, not a Village preference

Traffic Impact Study Review (Jan 9, 2026): ADA compliance must be demonstrated.

🔍 Tree Preservation Failures

The Village flagged tree preservation as a repeat comment across multiple review cycles—meaning the developer has failed to address it through successive submittals:

  • No proper tree survey submitted—what was provided is described as "a marked up demolition plan"
  • UDO requires tree survey as part of any PUD application
  • 15 plant units required; compliance not demonstrated
  • Evergreen trees shown at 6 ft height; requires 8 ft minimum
  • Shade trees shown at 2.5-inch caliper; requires 3-inch minimum
  • 52% open space claim includes areas UDO explicitly excludes from calculation

Village Planning Review (Feb 13, 2026): Repeat deficiency not corrected.

Why Should You Care?

🏡

Property Values

Rezoning decisions can significantly affect your home's value. Changes in neighborhood character, increased density, or altered traffic patterns often influence property values in surrounding areas.

🚗

Traffic & Infrastructure

Different zoning classifications bring different levels of traffic and infrastructure demands. Commercial or multi-family developments typically generate more vehicles, affecting congestion, street parking, and wear on local roads.

🌳

Neighborhood Character

Our surrounding neighborhoods were designed as single-family residential communities with specific aesthetic and lifestyle qualities. Rezoning can alter the visual landscape, green space, and the overall character that makes our area unique.

📊

Density & Privacy

Increased density from multi-family or commercial development can impact privacy, noise levels, and the peaceful residential atmosphere that residents chose when purchasing their homes in this area.

🏫

Local Services

Changes in zoning can affect demand on local schools, parks, utilities, and emergency services. Higher density developments may strain existing infrastructure and services.

Your Voice Matters

Public input during Planning & Zoning Commission meetings is a critical part of the decision-making process. Commissioners consider resident concerns when making recommendations to the Village Board.

How the Process Works

  • 1
    Application Submitted: A property owner or developer submits a rezoning request to the Village's Community Development Department.
  • 2
    Staff Review: Village staff analyzes the proposal, checking compliance with the Comprehensive Plan and evaluating potential impacts.
  • 3
    Public Hearing: The Planning & Zoning Commission holds a public hearing where residents can voice concerns, ask questions, and provide input.
  • 4
    Commission Recommendation: After considering public input and staff analysis, the Commission makes a recommendation to approve, deny, or modify the rezoning request.
  • 5
    Village Board Decision: The Village Board of Trustees makes the final decision on the rezoning proposal.
